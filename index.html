Bittensor Basics

Essential Bittensor terminology you need to understand before using MetaHash.

Œ± (Alpha) / Alpha Token

What it is: Subnet-specific reward tokens that represent emissions from a particular Bittensor subnet.

Real-world analogy: Like store-specific gift cards. Each subnet gives you their own "gift card" (Œ±), and MetaHash is the exchange where you can trade them for something more liquid (META).

Example: If you mine on Subnet 18, you earn "SN18 Œ±". If you mine on Subnet 5, you earn "SN5 Œ±". Each is different and has different value.

In MetaHash: You offer your Œ± in auctions to receive META tokens.

œÑ (Tau) / TAO

What it is: The native cryptocurrency of the Bittensor blockchain. Used for staking, governance, and paying transaction fees.

In MetaHash: You need small amounts (~0.1 œÑ) to pay for gas fees when submitting bids or making payments.

How to get: Purchase on exchanges like Binance, MEXC, or through OTC trades.

Subnet

What it is: An independent AI network running on Bittensor. Each subnet has a specific purpose or function.

  • Subnet 62: Software Engineering Agents

MetaHash-Specific Terms

Key terminology specific to MetaHash auctions, bids, and operations.

Weight Allocation

What it is: The mechanism by which validators distribute rewards to miners in MetaHash.

How it works:

  • Validators run deterministic auctions to allocate weights fairly
  • Miners bid for Œ± access and pay to whitelisted treasuries
  • Successful payments result in higher weight allocation
  • Weights are set on-chain after settlement (or preview-only in testing mode)

Key feature: Replaces subjective weight allocation with transparent, auction-based mechanisms.

Auction

What it is: A competitive bidding process that happens every tempo (360 blocks, ~72 minutes) where miners bid for Œ± access.

The process:

  1. Preparation: First 50 blocks of the tempo finalize prior rewards through Yuma Consensus and stage the new auction budget.
  2. Announcement: Validator broadcasts AuctionStart with discount caps and payment windows.
  3. Bidding: Miners submit bids (subnet, Œ± amount, discount) for the remainder of the tempo.
  4. Clearing: Final 20 blocks close bidding while validators rank bids deterministically.
  5. Payment: Winners send Œ± to whitelisted treasury addresses during the next tempo.
  6. Settlement: Validator verifies payments and sets weights on-chain two tempos later.

Bid

What it is: Your offer to obtain Œ± access in an auction by specifying the subnet, amount, and discount you're willing to offer.

Example: "I want 100 SN18-Œ± and will offer a 5% discount."

Strategy: Balance between competitive discounts (higher = more likely to win) and preserving value. Higher discounts make bids more competitive.

Discount (BPS)

What it is: The percentage discount you offer on your Œ± to make your bid competitive.

Format: Basis points (BPS) where 100 BPS = 1%.

Examples:

  • 500 BPS = 5% discount
  • 1000 BPS = 10% discount
  • 1500 BPS = 15% discount

Treasury

What it is: The official wallet address where miners must send Œ± payments after winning auctions.

Critical importance: Only whitelisted treasuries are valid. Sending to the wrong address leads to permanent loss of funds.

How to verify: Always check metahash/treasuries.py in the official repository.

Payment Window

What it is: The specific time period [start_block, end_block] during which you must send Œ± after winning an auction.

Example: [5931900, 5932000] means you have 100 blocks (~20 minutes) to pay.

Important: Set alerts‚Äîlate payments mean you lose your auction win and damage your reputation.

Reputation

What it is: A score assigned to each coldkey based on historical auction behavior.

Affects:

  • Maximum bid amount you can submit
  • Priority when bids have similar value
  • Trust level in the system

Build it: Win auctions and pay on time consistently.

Tempo (Epoch)

What it is: Bittensor‚Äôs scheduling unit. One tempo spans 360 blocks (~72 minutes) and is sometimes called an epoch in tooling.

In MetaHash:

  • Tempo T: Auction + Clearing phase
  • Tempo T+1: Payment window + Commitment phase
  • Tempo T+2: Settlement phase

Slippage

What it is: The price impact that occurs when trading large amounts of Œ± relative to available liquidity.

Example:

  • Small bid (100 Œ±): 0.5% slippage ‚Äî minimal impact
  • Large bid (10,000 Œ±): 15% slippage ‚Äî might be rejected!

How to avoid: Bid smaller amounts relative to subnet liquidity, or split into multiple bids.

How MetaHash Works: A Complete Overview

Understanding the full lifecycle from auction to settlement in the MetaHash protocol.

Reading Time: 10 minutes

Prerequisite: Basic understanding of Bittensor

The Problem We Solve

In the Bittensor ecosystem, there are two key challenges:

  • For miners & subnet operators: Need access to Œ± capital but can't acquire it without destabilizing existing liquidity pools.
  • For everyone: Subjective weight allocation by validators lacks transparency and fairness.

The MetaHash Solution

MetaHash creates a decentralized marketplace that connects capital holders with capital seekers through fair, deterministic auctions.

Think of it like this:

  • dTAO holders can deploy their idle Œ± across multiple subnets without mining.
  • Miners & subnet operators can bid for Œ± access to grow their operations.
  • Validators run transparent auctions with on-chain commitments and slippage-aware clearing.
  • Everyone benefits from fair price discovery and deterministic weight allocation.

Key Features

For dTAO Holders:

  • Avoid slippage: Deploy Œ± without destabilizing your original pools.

The Tempo-Based Auction Cycle

MetaHash operates on a continuous tempo. Each tempo spans 360 blocks (~72 minutes), with Yuma Consensus finalizing who earned the rewards every round.

Tempo T: Auction & Clearing

Phase 1: Preparation (blocks 0-50)

  • Validators finalize the prior tempo‚Äôs rewards through Yuma Consensus.
  • Budget, discount caps, and payment windows for the new tempo are staged.
  • State resets ensure everyone starts the auction from a clean slate.

Phase 2: Live bidding (blocks 50-340)

  • Validators broadcast AuctionStart once preparation completes.
  • Active miners submit bids each tempo: (subnet_id, Œ±_amount, discount).
  • Multiple bids per miner are allowed while the window is open.
  • Competition centers on best discounted value after slippage adjustments.

Phase 3: Close-out (blocks 340-360)

  • Bidding window closes; validators rank all bids by deterministic value.
  • Reputation limits and budget constraints determine the final winners.
  • Winning bids are paired with payment instructions for the next tempo.

Tempo T+1: Commitment & Payment

  • Winners notified: "You won! Pay by block X."
  • Validator publishes commitment (CID ‚Üí IPFS).
  • Payment window opens (typically 100 blocks).
  • Miners send Œ± to whitelisted treasury.
  • Transactions confirmed on-chain.

Tempo T+2: Settlement

  • Validator scans blockchain for Œ± payments.
  • Verifies: correct amount, treasury, timing.
  • Calculates miner scores (paid = high, missed = 0).
  • Burns underfilled budget to UID 0.
  • Sets weights on-chain (or preview-only in testing mode).
  • Emissions distributed based on weights.

Visual Summary: Auction (Tempo T) ‚Üí Payment (Tempo T+1) ‚Üí Settlement (Tempo T+2)

Example cadence: Blocks 0-50 preparation, 50-340 live bids, 340-360 close-out and winner selection.

Terminology bridge: Live dashboards label these phases as Accepting Bids (Tempo T), Payment (Tempo T+1), and Rewards / Finalization (Tempo T+2). Same workflow, different naming.

Key Mechanisms

Understanding the technical systems that make MetaHash fair, secure, and efficient.

1. Slippage-Aware Valuation

If you bid 10,000 Œ± but the subnet's liquidity pool only has $5,000 worth, your Œ± isn't actually worth face value. MetaHash calculates slippage based on bid size relative to liquidity to ensure fair valuation.

2. Reputation System

Prevents spam and ensures reliable participants:

  • Win + pay on time ‚Üí reputation increases.
  • Miss payment ‚Üí reputation decreases significantly.
  • Reputation affects maximum bid capacity.

3. Budget Signaling & Burns

Validators signal expected budget (bt_mu, bl_mu). If bids fall short, the difference is burned (sent to UID 0), creating deflationary pressure and maintaining system integrity through deterministic burns.

4. IPFS + On-Chain Commitments

Validators publish auction commitments with Content ID (CID) on-chain and full data to IPFS. This hybrid approach keeps on-chain storage minimal while maintaining transparency. Anyone can retrieve and verify auction results.

Security & Safety Features

  • Whitelisted Treasuries: Only approved addresses can receive Œ± payments.
  • Strict Payment Windows: Clear block-based deadlines prevent gaming.
  • Slippage Guards: Excessive slippage (>15% typically) results in bid rejection.
  • Deterministic Clearing: Same inputs always produce same outputs‚Äîno favoritism.
  • Reputation Caps: Limits exposure for new/untrusted coldkeys.
  • On-Chain Transparency: All commitments published with verifiable CIDs.
  • Testing Mode: Preview weights without on-chain submission for safe testing.

Validator Revenue Snapshot

Translate clearing volume into weekly earnings so you can plan infrastructure budgets.

Estimator formula

Use on-chain totals from the live auction dashboard.

weekly_revenue = (cleared_alpha * avg_discount) * validator_fee_share

Worked example:

  • Cleared Œ± per week: 48,000
  • Weighted discount: 8% (0.08)
  • Validator share: 35% (0.35)
  • Weekly revenue: 48,000 * 0.08 * 0.35 = 1,344 Œ±-equivalent

Convert to TAO or USD using current market prices to decide whether increasing slots or scaling failover hardware is justified.

Throughput checkpoints

  • Daily auctions: ~20 (72-minute cadence).
  • Average validators clearing: 1 primary + 1 standby.
  • Typical settlement burn: <5% of posted budget when payments complete.

Track your actual volumes by exporting the commitment CSVs published each epoch and aggregating per validator hotkey.

Miner ROI Playbook

Model return on Œ± allocations with different discount strategies and win rates.

ROI formula

roi = (meta_received - alpha_paid) / alpha_paid

Where alpha_paid = bid_alpha * (1 - discount_bps/10,000).

Scenarios

Strategy Bid Œ± Discount Meta received Alpha paid ROI
Conservative 2,000 5% (500 bps) 1,900 META 1,900 Œ± 0%
Competitive 2,000 10% (1,000 bps) 1,850 META 1,800 Œ± +2.78%
Aggressive 2,000 18% (1,800 bps) 1,760 META 1,640 Œ± +7.32%

Higher discounts win bids more reliably but compress ROI. Use recent clearing data to calibrate which subnets reward higher exposure.

Win-rate heuristics

  • Discounts <5% often require higher reputation to clear consistently.
  • 8‚Äì12% clears in most standard liquidity windows without hitting slippage caps.
  • >15% is reserved for urgent liquidity; expect lower net META but near-100% win rate.

Treasury Whitelisting Explained

Understanding MetaHash's security mechanism for protecting treasury funds

What is Treasury Whitelisting?

In MetaHash's governance model, treasury whitelisting is a security measure to protect the network's funds from malicious actors. It means that any destination account or payment address must be pre-approved (whitelisted) by trusted parties before it can receive Œ± payments from auction winners.

Critical Safety Rule: ONLY send Œ± to whitelisted treasury addresses! Sending to non-whitelisted addresses results in permanent loss of funds.

Why is Treasury Whitelisting Needed?

Without whitelisting, several risks could emerge:

  • Malicious actors could create fake treasury addresses to steal Œ± tokens.
  • Typos or errors could result in funds being sent to wrong addresses.
  • Social engineering attacks could trick users into sending to attacker-controlled addresses.
  • Governance exploits could potentially redirect treasury funds.

Whitelisting mitigates these risks by adding a checkpoint: only pre-approved addresses can receive treasury payments. If someone tries to send funds to an unrecognized address, validators will reject the payment in settlement.

How Does It Work?

The Two-Key Security Model

Think of treasury whitelisting like requiring two keys to open a safe:

  • Community's key: Validators run auctions and determine winners.
  • Foundation's key: Only whitelisted addresses are valid payment destinations.

Both must align for a payment to be considered valid. This creates a check-and-balance system.

Who Can Whitelist Addresses?

The MetaHash subnet owner and core team are responsible for managing the whitelist. Addresses are added through:

  • Governance proposals ‚Äî Community can propose new treasury addresses.
  • Core team approval ‚Äî Subnet owner verifies and adds legitimate addresses.
  • Multi-sig verification ‚Äî Multiple parties must confirm before whitelisting.

How to Verify Whitelisted Addresses

ALWAYS verify before sending Œ±! Follow these steps:

Step 1: Check the Official Repository

# Clone the repository
git clone https://github.com/fx-integral/metahash.git
cd metahash

# View whitelisted treasuries
cat metahash/treasuries.py

Step 2: Programmatically Confirm the Address

python -c "from treasuries import WHITELISTED_TREASURIES; addr='PASTE_ADDRESS_HERE'; print('‚úÖ Safe' if addr in WHITELISTED_TREASURIES else '‚ùå Not Whitelisted')"

Step 3: Validate with btcli

btcli treasury list --netuid 73
# Ensure the address in your invoice appears in this list exactly

Step 4: Cross-Reference Multiple Sources

  • Check the official MetaHash documentation.
  • Verify in the subnet's Discord channel.
  • Confirm with validator announcements on-chain.

Step 5: Test with Small Amount First

When using a treasury address for the first time:

  1. Send a small test amount (e.g., 1-10 Œ±).
  2. Wait for settlement to confirm it was accepted.
  3. Only then send larger amounts.

What Addresses Are Typically Whitelisted?

The whitelist typically includes:

  • MetaHash Foundation addresses ‚Äî For core development funding.
  • Subnet treasury addresses ‚Äî Where Œ± is pooled for distribution.
  • Community grant multisigs ‚Äî For approved community initiatives.
  • Validator reward pools ‚Äî For emission distribution.

Security Benefits

Defense Layers

  • Commitment proofs: Validators publish IPFS CIDs tied to each treasury, making settlement tampering detectable.
  • Multi-signature controls: Treasuries use multi-sig wallets so no single operator can drain funds.
  • Monitoring bots: Community scripts alert Discord if new, non-whitelisted addresses appear in settlement logs.

Protection Against Attacks

Even if a malicious actor somehow gained influence, they still could not:

  • Redirect funds to their own address (not whitelisted).
  • Create fake treasury addresses that validators would accept.
  • Drain the treasury through social engineering.

Transparency and Trust

  • On-chain verification: All treasury addresses are publicly visible.
  • Community oversight: Anyone can audit whitelisted addresses.
  • Deterministic validation: Validators automatically reject non-whitelisted addresses.

For Miners: Payment Best Practices

üîí Before Every Payment

  1. Verify the treasury address in treasuries.py.
  2. Check the subnet_id matches your bid.
  3. Confirm you're within the payment window.
  4. Send only after triple-checking the address.
  5. Save transaction hash for your records.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ‚ùå Copying address from unverified sources (Discord DMs, random websites).
  • ‚ùå Sending to addresses from old/outdated documentation.
  • ‚ùå Trusting addresses shared by other miners without verification.
  • ‚ùå Sending to validator addresses directly.
  • ‚úÖ ALWAYS check metahash/treasuries.py in official repo.

Governance and Evolution

Treasury whitelisting is not static. The community can:

  • Propose new addresses through governance.
  • Remove outdated addresses that are no longer needed.
  • Update mechanisms as the network matures.

This measure is in place not to centralize control, but to responsibly decentralize. As the community gains trust and the network matures, the whitelist can be expanded through governance votes.

Key Takeaway: Treasury whitelisting protects everyone. It ensures that Œ± payments go to legitimate addresses, maintaining trust in the MetaHash auction system. Always verify addresses in the official repository before sending any Œ± tokens.

Governance & Proposal Process

Learn how to propose treasury changes, participate in voting, and shepherd upgrades through MetaHash governance.

Phase 1

Prepare Your Proposal

Compile evidence and draft the change before submitting it to the community.

Checklist

  • Describe the change (e.g., add new treasury address, adjust auction parameter).
  • Provide justification: security review, stakeholder approvals, expected impact.
  • Include verification data (signatures, coldkey attestations, multisig details).
  • Draft success criteria and rollback plan.

Template

# Proposal Title
## Summary
- What change is being requested?
- Who benefits and why?

## Details
- Addresses involved
- Parameters affected
- Backwards compatibility

## Validation
- Signatures / reviewers
- Testnet or dry-run evidence

## Timeline
- Submission date
- Requested activation height/date

Share the draft in MetaHash Discord to collect early feedback before formal submission.

Phase 2

Submit & Vote

Move the proposal into the official pipeline and shepherd it through community approval.

Submission flow

  1. Open an issue or pull request in fx-integral/metahash referencing the proposal template.
  2. Announce in Discord (#governance) with a short summary and link.
  3. Tag validators and large miners for review; keep a Q&A thread for clarifications.

Voting timeline

  • Discussion window: 48 hours minimum for community questions.
  • Validator sign-off: 24-hour poll among active validators (simple majority).
  • Final confirmation: Document signatories and publish the activation block.

Track sign-offs in a shared spreadsheet or GitHub issue checklist. A proposal is considered approved when >50% of active validators and at least one foundation representative sign.

Phase 3

Activate & Monitor

Roll out the change and watch for regressions or follow-up actions.

Activation steps

  • Merge the approved change (e.g., new treasury entry) into the canonical repo.
  • Tag a release and notify validators to update before the activation block.
  • Publish the final CID or commit hash in Discord and pin for visibility.

Post-activation monitoring

  • Verify new treasury addresses receive and settle payments correctly.
  • Log any anomalies (missed payments, mislabeled invoices) within the first 24 hours.
  • If issues arise, execute the rollback plan documented in Phase 1.

Record keeping: Archive the proposal thread, validator votes, and activation confirmation. This builds precedent for future governance actions.

Need help? Reach out in Discord, or open a ‚ÄúGovernance Support‚Äù issue describing the proposal stage and blockers.

Error Reference: Common Issues & Solutions

Jump straight to specific errors or browse by category. Each entry includes root cause, fixes, and prevention tips.

Try keywords like slippage, btcli, treasury, or error codes.

Installation & Setup

ModuleNotFoundError: No module named 'bittensor'

Cause: Bittensor package missing from current environment.

Fix:

pip install bittensor --upgrade

Prevent: Activate your virtualenv before running MetaHash scripts (source venv/bin/activate).

Permission denied when accessing wallet

Cause: Wallet files created with restrictive or root ownership.

Fix:

chmod 600 ~/.bittensor/wallets/*/coldkey
chmod 600 ~/.bittensor/wallets/*/hotkeys/*

Prevent: Avoid running wallet commands with sudo; keep wallets owned by the runtime user.

Connection refused: port 9944

Cause: Cannot reach the Finney RPC endpoint.

Fix:

  1. Check network/firewall allows outbound 443.
  2. Swap endpoint: wss://entrypoint-finney.opentensor.ai:443.
  3. Validate DNS with nslookup entrypoint-finney.opentensor.ai.

Prevent: Keep a fallback endpoint handy and monitor latency with btcli net ping.

RuntimeError: Ledger device not found

Cause: Hardware wallet not connected, locked, or missing udev rules.

Fix:

  • Unlock Ledger and open the Bittensor app.
  • On Linux, install udev rules: sudo btcli ledger enable.
  • Reconnect USB and rerun the command.

Prevent: Keep firmware/apps updated and verify Ledger is detected with btcli ledger list.

Auction & Mining

Bid rejected: excessive slippage

Cause: Bid size exceeds current Œ± liquidity buffer.

Fix:

  • Reduce bid amount (start at 30-50% of pool depth).
  • Split orders across multiple auctions or subnets.
  • Inspect on-chain depth with btcli swap depth --netuid 73.

Prevent: Track liquidity snapshots and set conservative default discounts in config.json.

Bid rejected: hotkey exceeds reputation cap

Cause: Hotkey reputation too low for requested allocation.

Fix:

  • Fulfill smaller bids consistently to build reputation.
  • Check cap with btcli reputation show --hotkey YOUR_HOTKEY.
  • Split the bid into smaller chunks under the cap.

Prevent: Avoid missed payments and maintain activity each epoch.

Insufficient Œ± balance

Cause: Wallet balance lower than bid commitment.

Fix:

  • Confirm balance: btcli wallet overview --wallet.name YOUR_WALLET.
  • Mine additional Œ± or adjust bid size.
  • Ensure transfer confirmations before bidding again.

Prevent: Automate a pre-bid balance check in custom scripts.

AuctionCommitmentMismatch

Cause: Validator commitment CID differs from settlement data.

Log sample:

[ERROR] commitment CID mismatch | expected=QmX... | observed=QmY...

Fix:

  • Re-fetch commitments: btcli auction commitments --netuid 73.
  • Verify local cache cleared (rm ~/.metahash/cache/commitments*).
  • Contact validator if discrepancy persists.

Prevent: Keep node clocks in sync (enable systemd-timesyncd) and avoid editing downloaded commitment files.

Payments

Treasury address not whitelisted

Cause: Payment attempted to an address outside treasuries.py.

Fix:

# Verify address appears in the whitelist
python -c "from treasuries import WHITELISTED_TREASURIES; addr='PASTE'; print(addr in WHITELISTED_TREASURIES)"

Prevent: Validate every invoice through the Treasury Whitelisting checklist.

Payment window expired

Cause: Œ± transfer confirmed after the deadline.

Fix:

  • Bid again in the next auction cycle.
  • Notify validator to blacklist stale commitment.

Prevent: Set a timer when auction_start fires; keep a pre-filled transfer command ready.

Payment amount mismatch

Cause: Sent Œ± amount differs from winning invoice.

Fix:

  • Top up with the shortfall before the window closes.
  • Provide tx hash to validator for manual reconciliation.

Prevent: Copy-paste the exact amount string (including decimals) from the commitment file.

Network & System

SSLCertVerificationError: certificate verify failed

Cause: Local CA store outdated (common on macOS).

Fix:

# macOS Python bundle
/Applications/Python\ 3.x/Install\ Certificates.command

# Homebrew OpenSSL refresh
brew reinstall openssl

Prevent: Keep system packages updated and avoid MITM proxies.

TimeoutError: btcli rpc timed out

Cause: RPC endpoint not responding within default timeout.

Fix:

  • Retry with increased timeout: btcli --network finney --timeout 60 ...
  • Switch to a closer RPC endpoint.
  • Check local firewall or VPN latency.

Prevent: Monitor latency with btcli net latency and choose a stable endpoint.

ConnectionResetError / WSAECONNRESET

Cause: Remote peer closed the socket (network blip or validator restart).

Fix:

  • Allow the client to reconnect automatically.
  • Verify no local firewall is terminating idle connections.
  • Check validator status in btcli subnet list --netuid 73.

Prevent: Keep miners on reliable networks and enable automatic retries in supervisor scripts.

Validator Operations

No validator permit

Cause: Hotkey not in top stake or permit expired.

Fix:

  • Increase stake to re-enter top 64.
  • Wait for next immunities cycle if newly registered.
  • Confirm permit status: btcli subnet list --netuid 73.

Prevent: Maintain competitive stake and uptime; avoid extended downtime penalties.

Weights rejected: InvalidSignature

Cause: Validator weights signed with mismatched hotkey (usually wrong wallet path).

Fix:

  • Confirm --wallet.name and --wallet.hotkey match the registered permit.
  • Regenerate hotkey permissions if necessary: btcli wallet new_hotkey.
  • Restart validator with the correct config.

Prevent: Use explicit wallet paths in systemd unit files and avoid copying configs between environments without editing.

Payment verification failed: transaction not found

Cause: Validator cannot find Œ± transfer on-chain within the settlement window.

Fix:

  • Double-check tx hash via btcli chain get_transaction --hash HASH.
  • Confirm the transfer reached the correct treasury and amount.
  • Update auction records to mark commitment as unpaid.

Prevent: Monitor treasury incoming transfers with a watcher script and enforce payment confirmations before closing windows.

Permit revoked: stake below minimum threshold

Cause: Coldkey stake dipped below the active validator cut-off (top-64) due to withdrawals, slashes, or new entrants.

Fix:

  • Top up stake via delegations or direct bond until you re-enter the top-64.
  • Wait for the next permit refresh epoch (~24h) after increasing stake.
  • Verify eligibility: btcli subnet list --netuid 73 --show-permits.

Prevent: Maintain a buffer above the minimum stake, and set alerts for large delegation withdrawals hitting your coldkey.

Commitment CID mismatch detected

Cause: The CID posted on-chain does not match the CID generated from your local commitment payload (often due to editing files after publishing).

Fix:

  • Rebuild the commitment payload and recompute the CID locally.
  • Re-publish the commitment to IPFS and update the on-chain CID.
  • Ensure no automation modifies the JSON between signing and upload.

Prevent: Treat commitment outputs as immutable artifacts‚Äîstore them in write-protected directories and include a checksum test in your CI pipeline.

Treasury address misconfiguration

Cause: Validator served invoices with an address not present in treasuries.py or approved governance state.

Fix:

  • Immediately pause new auctions and notify miners of the incorrect address.
  • Update your local treasuries.py and configuration to the whitelisted set.
  • Audit recent payments and reimburse any miners that paid incorrect addresses.

Prevent: Automate a pre-auction check that hashes the configured treasuries and compares them against the canonical repository before every cycle.
